<h2 class="RwFIZ sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="18frb" indentation="0" level="2" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">The scanning process</span></h2>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="2d2qr" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><strong data-mce-fragment="1">When using photogrammetry, it is absolutely crucial to prepare the object's surface, so that there are <u data-mce-fragment="1">thousands </u>(!) of tiny dots. If your object does not have a feature-rich surface (meaning: thousands of tiny dots), all other settings like lighting, background, camera and software won't help to achieve good or even any results. Please see </strong><a class="Y-s64 XzMaT" data-hook="WebLink" data-mce-fragment="1" data-mce-href="https://openscan-org.github.io/OpenScan-Doc/photogrammetry/basics/" href="https://openscan-org.github.io/OpenScan-Doc/photogrammetry/basics/" target="_blank"><strong data-mce-fragment="1"><u data-mce-fragment="1">this guide on GitHub</u></strong></a><strong data-mce-fragment="1"> for more details.</strong></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="67iv2" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Here is a close-up example of a well-prepared surface:</span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures05-600x600.webp" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="c3ofd" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Note, that primed surface plus one contrasting color of fine dots would already suffice</span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures06-600x600.webp" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="717ns" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Unfortunately, the surface preparation is not fully shown in the video. But scanning a bare and clean plastic miniature is de facto impossible with photogrammetry without using prior surface treatment. So you should either use an airbrush or one of the self-vanishing scanning sprays to prepare the surface accordingly.</span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="ckfae" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Some more details about the scanning process:</span></p>
<ul>
<li>the polarizer helps to eliminate specular highlights on the model and thus improve the quality of the output</li>
<li>the miniature is placed on one of the included holders and fixed with a little bit of putty</li>
<li>the OpenScanCloud can be used free-of-charge to create the resulting 3d models. The captured photos get transferred from the Raspberry Pi to the OpenScanCloud Servers for automated processing</li>
<li>The resulting, textured mesh will need manual scaling and minor mesh repair (couple of clicks in Meshmixer)</li>
<li>The ~80mm miniature is at the size limit of the <a class="Y-s64 XzMaT" data-hook="WebLink" data-mce-fragment="1" data-mce-href="https://www.openscan.eu/openscan-mini" href="https://www.openscan.eu/openscan-mini" target="_blank"><u data-mce-fragment="1">OpenScan Mini</u></a> and alternatively the <a class="Y-s64 XzMaT" data-hook="WebLink" data-mce-fragment="1" data-mce-href="https://www.openscan.eu/openscan-classic" href="https://www.openscan.eu/openscan-classic" target="_blank"><u data-mce-fragment="1">OpenScan Classic</u></a> could be used.</li>
</ul>
<h2 class="RwFIZ sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="89g99" indentation="0" level="2" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">The printing process</span></h2>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="803vr" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">An 8K LCD resin printer has been used. The Phrozen Sonic mini 8K has a pixel size of 20 &micro;m, which is slowly contributing to the overall "washed out" look of the results. Another factor is the usez z-height of roughly 50 &micro;m. </span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="nnmk" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Resin printing is a topic for itself and there are several factors that can influence the results. I will discuss some effects in the following section.</span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="dvfje" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]">&nbsp;</p>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="f9cp5" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(1) softer details at the bottom</span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures07-600x600.webp" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="4g0ts" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">The stones on the base seem a bit softer than the rest of the model. This was caused by the scanning process, as the limited depth of field of the camera can not capture the whole model (thus the size limit of 80-90 mm for the OpenScan Mini). </span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="chces" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">It is possible to improve such areas by using manual focus or even the focus stacking mode, which will create photos with a different, manually set, focal plane.</span></p>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="af5sb" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(2) printing artifacts - layering</span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures08-600x600.webp" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="eg2pk" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">LCD resin printers can produce very fine, visually pleasing results. Still, there are printing artifacts which will be barely visible. The X-Y inaccuracy (due to the pixel size) is relatively soft, whereas the discrete z-heights will produce a notable pattern/layers.</span></p>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="cmbol" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(3) hand connected to leg (after 6 iterations)</span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures09-600x600.webp"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="e7njl" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">There might be various reasons for this effect:</span></p>
<ul>
<li>occluded areas/narrow gaps are hard to scan - increasing the image number might help</li>
<li>it is not clear, whether this area was prepared properly. When using the airbrush/scan spray, it is very important not to miss any part of the visible surface.</li>
<li>Defects like this can be smoothed/removed relatively easily with a tool like Meshmixer, Blender or ZBrush (but anyway, it is better to avoid artifacts like this by improving the scanning procedure ;)</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="3trl3" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(4) growing base</span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures10-600x600.webp" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="epukt" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">Again, this is caused by the limited depth of field. The scanner is not able to capture details in the lowest areas due to the size of the model. The meshing algorithm will try to close those missing information, thus it will add some volume with each iteration.</span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="fo36l" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">There is a relatively simple solution: It is possible to re-orient the model and run a second scan. The second pass should cover those areas, which were not included in the first scan. The software should automatically stitch both scans.</span></p>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="5qngf" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(5) Inward bending sword/perspective warp</span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures11-600x600.webp"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="3h96q" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">In my opinion, this effect might have two causes:</span></p>
<ul>
<li>The orientation of the print on the platform might introduce some forces during the release cycle (z-movement). This could slightly bend the sword inwards (which corresponds to the z-direction of the printing process).</li>
<li>Lens distortion in the scanners' camera might also increase this effect.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="vGBkk sWzLp" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="6as30" indentation="0" level="3" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">(6) Scan failure </span></h3>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1"><img alt="" src="/assets/img/posts/2022-11-29-3d-scanning-miniatures/2022-11-29-scanning-miniatures12-600x600.webp"/></span></p>
<p class="j1LEL va-er" data-mce-fragment="1" dir="auto" id="6b93v" indentation="0" textstyle="[object Object]"><span data-mce-fragment="1">The result is barely recognizable, but the scanner only captured the view from the top. This happens occasionally, when the starting position of the scanner is too high and the motor runs off the gears. Easy fix: repeat the scan ;)</span></p>
